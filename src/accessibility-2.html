Accessibility - part 2

In the previous part of this article I dove into accessibility of the web for deaf and blind people. In this article I continueau on this subject but this time I'm researching the disabilities colorblindness and motor skill disorders.

Colorbind people

1 of 12 men are color blind. At women this is 1 of 250 (Astrid-d-g, 2016). This is an enourmous amount of people with a disabilitie. There are multiple types of color blindness:

Monochromacy

The biggest obstacle of being color blind in combination with the web is where states are only expressed using color. For example when the user choses a answer in a quiz and it highlights only in red or green. If there is no other indencation if the answer is right or wrong it will be very hard for the user to understand it. If the designer or developer only added an icon with for example a checkmark it would be much better. Also it's important to take into account the contrast of colors for people that suffer colorblindness.

People with poor motor skills

An other interesetting group of people are those with a motor skill disorder. They often can't control their hands or arms like "normal" people can do. So this makes it very hard to control their mouse. A lot of the web depends on users using their mouse as main input device. You might think about the tab key right now, but the fact is that many designers and developers don't style these and sometimes even remove them.

Marijn, the person that I spoke about his disorder and using the web with it, mostly uses his spacebar to navigate trough websites. The spacebar scrolls the website for a large part to next viewport. If he wants to scroll only a bit he uses his arrow keys. Clicking on items he still did with his mouse.

This is a way of navigating that he finds most quick. He doesn't even bother using the tab key anymore because of the lacking focus states.

Also, by default, a lot elements can be dragged in a browser. For most people this isn't a problem and it can come in handy. However when your arm or hand moves a lot it might be hard to click on those elements.

Conclusion

The web is place where people with a disability shouldn't be limited. It was build with accessibility in mind. In reality this isn't the case and often it's still not a very good expierence to browse the web for these people. Developers and designers should be more aware about these users. A good tool to test your websites as if you has one of these disabilities is Funkify. Funkify aims to simulate certain disorders and you can toggle it in Chrome. But keep in mind that you can turn it off at any time but those people can't.
